Mandatory Exhibit: Civilization Timeline

Key political, economic, military, cultural, and architectural achievements/events.

330 CE

Founding of Constantinople

The Roman Emperor Constantine the Great announces Byzantium as the new capital city of the Eastern Roman Empire and changes its name to Constantinople. This city becomes important for trade and defense purposes.

527 CE

Coronation of Justinian I

Justinian I becomes the emperor together with Empress Theodora, ushering in the period of prosperity and growth of territory for the civilization that made it among.

529 CE

Publication of the Justinian Code

The legal commission produces Corpus Juris Civilis, which provides order to several centuries of disorganized Roman laws by codifying them into a systematic set of laws.

532 CE

The Nika Riots

Unrest develops into a full-scale uprising in the Hippodrome, threatening to tear the city apart. The emperor puts down the rebellion, ensuring total dominance over the empire. Economics collapse; everyone despises him.

537 CE

Completion of the Hagia Sophia

Built within only five years after the Nika Revolt, this architectural wonder contains a dome that is unlike anything else, functioning as the epicenter for Orthodox Christianity. One of the earliest civilizations that adopted Christianity as its religion.

674–678 CE

First Arab Siege of Constantinople

Byzantines manage to fend off an intense naval assault from the Umayyad Caliphate, which is mostly attributed to their effective use of their newfound and highly destructive weapon called “Greek Fire.”

726 CE

Start of the Iconoclastic Controversy

Leo III, the Byzantine Emperor, orders the destruction of religious icons, leading to more than one hundred years of unrest and the breakdown of relations between Byzantium and the West as well as Asia.

1054 CE

The Great Schism

The Christian world was permanently split into Roman Catholicism and Eastern Orthodoxy with mutual excommunications between the Pope in Rome and the Patriarch in Constantinople.

1204 CE

The Fourth Crusade Sack of Constantinople

Western European crusaders, diverted from their original goal, attack and sack Constantinople. They set up a short-lived Latin Empire, and cripple Byzantine wealth, military strength, and infrastructure for good.

1453 CE

Fall of Constantinople

Ottoman Sultan Mehmed II uses gunpowder artillery to break through the ancient walls of the city. The last emperor, Constantine XI, dies, marking the formal demise of the Byzantine Empire.

Ethical Judgment: Historical Villain

The Accused: Emperor Justinian I

From 527 to 565 CE, Emperor Justinian I presided over a time of great architectural achievement, growth in land area, and changes in law; however, high taxation and an autocratic form of government led to dissatisfaction among the populace, culminating in the Nika Riots of 532 CE.

Initially panicking during the Riot, Justinian got ready to leave the city. In response to an unexpected show of courage from Empress Theodora, he altered his plan of escape and sent generals into the Hippodrome to confront the hundreds of thousands of citizens that had gathered there in protest. The military then locked down the compound and systematically executed more than 30,000 unarmed or inadequately armed individuals. This was an intentional large-scale militarised state's attempt to kill as many citizens dissenting from the ruling authority of Justinian as possible, to keep him in power by force, and would therefore be deemed by present-day international human rights laws as an absolute war crime.

Historical Significance: Great Inventions

Three innovations that transformed Byzantine society and its various social classes.

Significance: A top secret, chemical weapon that would burn on water and couldn't be put out by most means, shot out through siphons on warships.

Social Group Impact: This had preserved the survival of the empire’s ruling and merchant classes by successfully saving Constantinople against multiple maritime invasions, securing maritime trade routes for centuries.

Significance: The structural innovation of placing a massive circular dome over a square room by using triangular support pillars (pendentives), famously utilized in the Hagia Sophia.

Social Group Impact: It had deeply impacted the religious and poor classes, fostering an intense spiritual awe. The engineering made churchgoers feel as the physical ceiling was a literal gateway from heaven.

Significance: A massive collection of organization, and modernization of hundreds of years of chaotic Roman laws and legal opinions was put into a single, organized civil code (Corpus Juris Civilis).

Social Group Impact: It had streamlined the judicial process for lawyers, bureaucrats, and judges, creating a more predictable and uniform system of justice, property rights, and contracts for upper- and middle-class citizens and poor citizens.

Historical Perspective: The Good & The Bad

The extensive preservation of classical antiquity. While Western Europe had gone through centuries of political fragmentation and lost touch with past scholarship, Byzantine scholars, scribes, and monastics was meticulously copied, preserved, and analyzed ancient Greek and Roman philosophy, science, drama, and literature.

The common political practice of facial mutilation (rhinotomy and blinding). Because a Byzantine emperor was culturally required to be physically not having defects to reflect the best perfection, deposed emperors or political rivals had their noses sliced off, tongues cut out, or eyes permanently blinded to disqualify them from ever holding power again.

From a modern humanitarian standpoint, political mutilation is viewed as a horrific, cruel, and unusual violation of basic human dignity. However, a personal bias might overlook that the Byzantines viewed this practice as a more "merciful" alternative to standard execution, believing it had avoided unnecessary bloodshed while still maintaining good political stability.

Cause & Consequence: Explaining the Fall

1. Economic Decline and Feudalization: Over centuries, wealthy landed aristocrats (the Dynatoi) seized land from the poor soldier class, draining the empire's taxes and destroying the local military recruitment system.

2. The Fourth Crusade (1204 CE): Western European Crusaders completely devastated the capital, looting its wealth, destroying its infrastructure, and making wars in its territories. The empire could never truly recover its economy or population.

3. Constant Border Warfare: The empire was trapped in perpetual, multi-front wars against the Bulgarians and Serbians in Europe, and Arab caliphates followed by the Seljuk Turks in Asia Minor, gradually taking away at its borders.

1. Ottoman Military Superiority and Gunpowder: Sultan Mehmed II had brought many advanced, giant bronze cannons capable of firing massive stone balls that could obliterate the ancient, thrice-layered Theodosian Walls.

2. Total Isolation: The Byzantines had appealed to Western European nations for military aid failed due to lingering religious animosity from the Great Schism, leaving fewer than 8,000 defenders to hold off an Ottoman army of over 80,000.

3. The Final Breach: On May 29, 1453, Ottoman forces launched a coordinated final assault, successfully finding a unlocked postern gate (Kerkoporta) and overwhelming the already exhausted defenders.

Change & Continuity: Before & After

Momentous Turning Point: The Iconoclastic Controversy (726–843 CE)

It caused massive internal social and religious turmoil, turning the state military against its own citizens and the monastic communities. It had resulted in the destruction of centuries of religious art and severely fractured geopolitical relations between the Byzantine emperors and the Popes in Rome, accelerating the alignment of the Western Papacy with Charlemagne and the Francs.

The relationship between the state and religious visual culture shifted profoundly. It had redefined the boundary of the emperor's power over church doctrine and temporarily split the population into opposing theological factions (Iconoclasts vs. Iconophiles), forever changing the course of Christian art.

Despite decades of violent debate over how religious images should be viewed, the absolute theological structure of Eastern Orthodox Christianity had been intact, the supremacy of the emperor over church affairs had remained undisputed, and religious devotion continued to be part of a daily civic life.

Evidence & Interpretation: "The Write Stuff"

Writing in the Byzantine Empire consisted of Medieval Greek, written in both formal uncial (capital letters) and later minuscule cursive scripts on parchment, vellum, and occasionally imported papyrus.

Writing was heavily utilized by the extensive imperial bureaucracy to enforce the centralized tax records, issue the complex legal decrees, and maintain administrative oversight across the distant provinces. It was also vital to keep the theological scholarship, preserving Christian orthodox doctrine against heresies, and copying ancient classical texts. The effect was a highly literate, intensely centralized administration that allowed a relatively small ruling class in Constantinople to manage and preserve a vast, multi-ethnic empire for over a millennium.
